eli-neutral: Welcome back to WiredWorkshop.
eli-neutral: In this session, we’re going to explore laptop hardware.
eli-neutral: Laptops are the standard computing device for anyone who needs mobility.
eli-neutral: They pack a lot of technology into a very small case, which makes them incredibly useful.
eli-neutral: But there’s a trade-off: limited space, limited upgradability, and sometimes much harder repairs.
eli-neutral: Understanding what’s inside a laptop is essential for troubleshooting, upgrading, and passing your certification exams.

eli-neutral: Let’s begin with one of the most important components: the battery.
eli-neutral: Laptops rely on rechargeable batteries so you can use them on the go.
eli-neutral: You’ll see two main types: lithium-ion and lithium-ion polymer.
eli-neutral: Both avoid the old memory effect, so you can recharge anytime without damaging capacity.
eli-neutral: Some models use modular, removable batteries that pop out with a latch for fast replacement.
eli-neutral: Others, especially modern ultrabooks, embed internal batteries inside the chassis.
eli-neutral: Replacing internal batteries usually means opening the laptop and sometimes taking it almost completely apart.
eli-neutral: Battery shapes and sizes vary by manufacturer, so always match the exact model when ordering replacements.

eli-neutral: Another heavily used component is the keyboard.
eli-neutral: Keyboards are among the most replaced parts on laptops.
eli-neutral: Many are simple to swap: remove a bezel or a few screws, disconnect the ribbon cable, and drop in a new unit.
eli-neutral: For troubleshooting, connect an external USB keyboard to isolate OS versus hardware issues.
eli-neutral: Laptop keyboards are compact, so many functions are combined behind a function key.
eli-neutral: Larger laptops may include a numeric keypad; most smaller systems do not.
eli-neutral: Individual keycaps are fragile compared to desktop keys—follow manufacturer instructions if you replace them.

eli-neutral: Inside the system you’ll find memory, or RAM.
eli-neutral: Desktops use full-sized DIMMs, but laptops use smaller SO-DIMMs: small outline dual in-line memory modules.
eli-neutral: Many laptops provide a bottom access panel for quick RAM upgrades.
eli-neutral: Thin and light systems may solder RAM to the motherboard, preventing upgrades.
eli-neutral: To install a SO-DIMM, slide it in at an angle and press down until the side clips latch.

eli-neutral: Storage has evolved significantly.
eli-neutral: Older laptops used two-and-a-half-inch spinning hard drives—smaller cousins of desktop drives.
eli-neutral: Today, laptops almost always use SSDs for speed, durability, and reliability.
eli-neutral: SSDs appear in two main form factors: two-and-a-half-inch SATA drives and compact M.2 cards.
eli-neutral: M.2 drives plug directly into the motherboard and secure with a single screw.
eli-neutral: Upgrading to an SSD is one of the biggest performance boosts for older laptops.
eli-neutral: You can perform a clean OS install or use cloning software to migrate the existing system.

eli-neutral: Connectivity is another key part of laptop hardware.
eli-neutral: Many modern laptops omit a wired Ethernet port and rely on wireless radios.
eli-neutral: Wi-Fi is usually integrated into the system board, but some laptops use modular Mini PCIe or M.2 cards.
eli-neutral: These cards attach to tiny black and gray antenna leads that route through the display for better signal.
eli-neutral: Bluetooth is also common and supports short-range devices like mice, keyboards, headsets, or tethering.

eli-neutral: Security features increasingly include biometrics.
eli-neutral: Fingerprint readers and facial recognition can tie into Windows Hello for quick, secure logins.
eli-neutral: Hardware support is required—either a built-in fingerprint reader or a camera capable of face recognition.

eli-neutral: Some laptops support NFC—near field communication—for short range authentication.
eli-neutral: In enterprise settings, users may tap a badge against a laptop or dock to log in quickly and securely.

eli-neutral: Almost every laptop includes a built-in webcam and microphone.
eli-neutral: If quality is insufficient—or missing entirely—an external USB webcam adds simple plug-and-play video.

eli-neutral: Repairability and upgradeability vary widely across models.
eli-neutral: Some designs prioritize serviceability with easy access to batteries, RAM, and storage.
eli-neutral: Others are sealed tightly and difficult to open, especially thin-and-light systems.
eli-neutral: Bulkier business laptops often make upgrades easier.
eli-neutral: Always check the manufacturer’s service documentation before attempting repairs or upgrades.

eli-neutral: To sum up, laptops balance portability with limits on repair and expansion.
eli-neutral: We reviewed batteries, keyboards, memory, storage, wireless radios, biometrics, NFC, and webcams.
eli-neutral: Knowing what’s inside—and how it fits together—helps on certification exams and in real-world troubleshooting.
